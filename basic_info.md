## What is PM2.5?

PM2.5 refers to **fine inhalable particulate matter with a diameter of 2.5 micrometers or less**, which is roughly 30 times smaller than the width of a human hair. Due to their extremely small size, these particles can penetrate deep into the lungs and even enter the bloodstream.

Long-term or high-level exposure to PM2.5 is associated with serious health impacts, including:
- Cardiovascular diseases
- Lung cancer
- Respiratory illnesses
- Increased risk of stroke

PM2.5 pollution is a major concern in urban and industrial regions and is widely used as a key indicator of air quality.

---

## Sources of PM2.5

PM2.5 originates from both direct emissions and chemical processes in the atmosphere.

### 1. Combustion Sources
- Vehicle exhaust, especially diesel engines  
- Industrial emissions and power plants  
- Biomass burning, including wood stoves and wildfire smoke  

### 2. Natural and Human Activities
- Dust from construction sites  
- Agricultural activities  
- Open burning of waste  

### 3. Secondary Formation
- PM2.5 can also form in the atmosphere when gases such as sulfur dioxide (SO₂), nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), and ammonia react chemically under suitable conditions.

---

## Healthy PM2.5 Levels

A **healthy PM2.5 concentration** is generally considered to be **below 12 µg/m³** (micrograms per cubic meter) when measured as an annual average.

Values above this threshold indicate increasing health risks, particularly for children, the elderly, and individuals with pre-existing respiratory or cardiovascular conditions.
